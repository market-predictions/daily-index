## 5. Key risks / invalidators

- A rapid de-escalation in the Middle East and sharp oil reversal would reduce the USD / JPY / CHF defensive premium.
- A decisive broadening of non-US growth plus faster Fed easing would weaken the USD-overweight case.
- A material improvement in euro-area growth with no renewed energy pressure would improve EUR.
- A sharper-than-expected BoJ dovish turn would weaken the JPY thesis.
- A cleaner global risk-on reset would help AUD, NZD and ZAR faster than this framework currently assumes.

## 6. Bottom line

The inaugural portfolio should be positioned for **defense first, optionality second, high-beta last**. In practice that means a **high USD cash weight**, a **meaningful JPY sleeve**, a **defensive CHF allocation**, and only selective exposure to cyclical or carry currencies.

## 10. Current currency review

**USD — Buy**  
Fed policy remains at 3.50–3.75%, growth is still described as solid, and the Fed explicitly notes elevated uncertainty and Middle East risks. In a regime where oil, geopolitics and trade-policy uncertainty all matter at once, USD remains the highest-quality base sleeve.

**EUR — Reduce**  
The ECB is already down to a 2.00% deposit rate and remains data-dependent. The euro area is resilient, but its cyclical repair is not strong enough to offset lower relative rates and renewed energy sensitivity.

**JPY — Buy**  
BoJ policy is still low in absolute terms, but no longer ultra-zero. That means JPY retains haven characteristics while gradually losing part of the old policy drag that used to cap it.

**CHF — Build on weakness**  
CHF remains a clean defensive diversifier. The SNB policy rate is only 0.00%, so CHF is not a carry currency, but in a geopolitical hedge regime that matters less than its defensive role.

**GBP — Hold**  
Bank Rate at 3.75% still gives GBP rate support, and the BoE continues to sound cautious about inflation persistence. But the UK growth backdrop is soft and labour-market slack is building, which limits upside.

**AUD — Reduce**  
The RBA is relatively hawkish at 3.85%, which prevents a bearish collapse in AUD. But AUD is still a cyclical, China- and growth-sensitive currency, and that is not the leadership regime today.

**CAD — Hold**  
CAD gets a positive oil and terms-of-trade impulse, which is important in the current shock. The offset is Canada’s still-modest domestic growth and continuing exposure to US trade-policy uncertainty.

**NZD — Sell / avoid**  
The RBNZ is at 2.25% and expects inflation to fall, with the labour market still weak. That leaves NZD as a lower-priority cyclical recovery story rather than a top-tier sleeve.

**MXN — Hold**  
Banxico still offers 7.00% policy carry, which keeps MXN investable. But in a mild risk-off regime the correct posture is selective holding, not aggressive adding.

**ZAR — Sell / avoid**  
SARB still offers a high 6.75% repo rate and inflation remains contained, but ZAR is the wrong kind of exposure for a portfolio that is currently optimized for defense, funding stress and oil/geopolitical uncertainty.
